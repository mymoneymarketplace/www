How to Choose a 0% APR Card

Key factors to consider before you apply.

1

Decide: Purchases or Balance Transfer

If you need to finance a large purchase interest-free, look for the longest 0% intro APR on purchases. If you want to pay down existing debt, focus on the longest 0% APR on balance transfers. Some cards offer 0% on both, but the intro periods may differ. Match the card type to your specific financial goal.

2

Calculate Your Monthly Payment

Divide the total amount you plan to charge or transfer by the number of months in the intro period. This gives you the monthly payment needed to pay off the entire balance before interest kicks in. If the monthly payment is too high, look for a card with a longer intro period to spread the payments out further.

3

Consider Rewards During 0% Period

Some 0% APR cards also earn rewards on your spending. The Chase Freedom Unlimited earns 1.5% cash back while offering 15 months at 0% APR. This lets you earn money back on purchases you are already financing interest-free, adding extra value during the introductory period.

0% APR Credit Card FAQs

Answers to the most common questions about 0% APR cards.

What is a 0% APR credit card?
A 0% APR credit card offers an introductory period during which you pay no interest on purchases, balance transfers, or both. These intro periods typically last 12 to 21 months, depending on the card. After the intro period ends, the card standard variable APR applies to any remaining balance. These cards are ideal for financing large purchases or paying down existing credit card debt.
How long do 0% APR offers last?
The longest 0% APR offers currently last up to 21 months for balance transfers on cards like the Wells Fargo Reflect and Citi Diamond Preferred. For new purchases, most 0% APR periods last 15 to 18 months. Some cards offer 0% on both purchases and balance transfers simultaneously, though the intro periods may differ for each.
What is the difference between 0% APR on purchases vs balance transfers?
A 0% APR on purchases means you pay no interest on new purchases made with the card during the intro period. A 0% APR on balance transfers means you pay no interest on debt moved from another card to this one. Some cards offer 0% on both, while others specialize in one or the other. Check the card terms to confirm which type of 0% APR you are getting.
Can I use a 0% APR card for a large purchase?
Yes, a 0% APR purchase card is an excellent way to finance a large purchase interest-free. For example, a $3,000 purchase on a card with 15 months at 0% APR lets you pay $200 per month with zero interest. Just make sure you can pay off the full balance before the intro period ends to avoid interest on the remaining amount.
What happens after the 0% APR period ends?
After the intro period, any remaining balance is subject to the card standard variable APR, which typically ranges from 17% to 29% depending on your creditworthiness. Interest begins accruing on the remaining balance immediately. This is why it is critical to pay off as much as possible during the 0% period.
Does a 0% APR card affect my credit score?
Applying for a 0% APR card triggers a hard inquiry that may lower your score by 5 to 10 points temporarily. However, if you use the card to pay down debt or maintain a low utilization ratio, the long-term impact on your credit score is typically positive. The key is making all payments on time and keeping your overall credit utilization below 30%.
Can I get rewards on a 0% APR card?
Some 0% APR cards also earn rewards. The Chase Freedom Unlimited offers 1.5% cash back on all purchases plus 15 months at 0% APR, and the Discover it Cash Back offers 5% rotating categories with 15 months at 0% on purchases. However, cards focused primarily on 0% APR for balance transfers, like the Citi Diamond Preferred, typically do not offer a rewards program.
What credit score do I need for a 0% APR card?
Most 0% APR cards with the longest intro periods require a good to excellent credit score of 670 or higher. The best offers, including those with 21-month intro periods, generally require scores of 700 or above. If your score is lower, you may still qualify for a card with a shorter intro period or a lower credit limit.
