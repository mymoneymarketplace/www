How to Choose a No Foreign Fee Card

Key factors for international spending.

1

Check Network Acceptance

Visa and Mastercard are accepted in more countries and at more merchants than American Express or Discover. If you travel to regions with limited card acceptance, prioritize a Visa or Mastercard no-FTF card as your primary international spending card and use Amex as a supplement where accepted.

2

Always Pay in Local Currency

When a merchant offers to charge you in US dollars instead of the local currency, decline and pay in the local currency. This practice, called dynamic currency conversion, adds a markup of 3% to 7% on top of your purchase. Paying in local currency ensures you get the competitive exchange rate from your card network.

3

Notify Your Issuer Before Travel

Some card issuers may flag or decline transactions that suddenly appear in a foreign country. Set a travel notification through your card issuer's app or website before departing. Most major issuers like Chase and Capital One no longer require travel notices, but it is worth confirming to avoid disruptions.

No Foreign Fee Card FAQs

Common questions about international card fees.

What is a foreign transaction fee?
A foreign transaction fee is a surcharge, typically 3% of the purchase amount, that your credit card issuer adds to any transaction made in a foreign currency or processed by a foreign bank. This fee applies to purchases made abroad and sometimes to online purchases from international merchants.
What is the best no foreign transaction fee card?
The Chase Sapphire Preferred is the best overall no foreign transaction fee card thanks to its combination of no FTF, 3x points on travel and dining, transfer partners, and a strong welcome bonus. The Capital One Venture X is another top choice with 2x miles on everything and premium travel perks.
Do all travel credit cards waive foreign transaction fees?
Most travel credit cards waive foreign transaction fees, but not all of them do. Always check the card terms before traveling internationally. Some cash back cards and general rewards cards also waive FTFs, so it is not limited to travel-specific cards.
Does Discover charge foreign transaction fees?
No, Discover does not charge foreign transaction fees on any of its cards. However, Discover has limited international acceptance compared to Visa and Mastercard, so it is best to carry a backup card when traveling abroad in case a merchant does not accept Discover.
Do foreign transaction fees apply to online purchases?
Yes, foreign transaction fees can apply to online purchases if the merchant is based outside of the United States or the transaction is processed in a foreign currency. This can happen when buying from international retailers, booking hotels abroad, or subscribing to services based overseas.
How much can I save by avoiding foreign transaction fees?
If you spend $3,000 on a two-week international trip, a 3% foreign transaction fee would cost you $90. Frequent travelers who spend $10,000 or more abroad annually save $300 or more just by using a card with no FTF. The savings increase significantly for extended trips or international business spending.
Is the exchange rate different with no-FTF cards?
No, the exchange rate is determined by the card network (Visa, Mastercard, or Amex) and is generally very close to the interbank rate. With a no-FTF card, you get this competitive exchange rate without any additional surcharge, making it the most cost-effective way to pay abroad.
Should I pay in local currency or US dollars when abroad?
Always pay in the local currency when using a no-FTF card. If a merchant offers to convert the charge to US dollars, this is called dynamic currency conversion and typically comes with a markup of 3% to 7%. By paying in local currency, you get the better exchange rate from your card network.
