What to Know

How We Picked the Best Cards for Travelers

We compared annual fees, welcome bonuses, category earn rates, and fine print across every mainstream issuer, then filtered to cards whose bonus categories align with how travelers typically spend. Every card on this list earns at least 2% effective cash-back return at realistic monthly spend.

Match the Card to Real Spending, Not the Marketing

The best card for travelers is not the one with the flashiest welcome bonus -- it is the one that earns the most on your actual monthly spend. Pull up the last three months of statements, sum spend by category, and pick the card whose multiplier aligns with your biggest line items. If your spending is spread evenly, a flat-rate 2% card wins.

Responsible Use and Credit-Score Impact

Credit cards help your score when you pay the full balance every month and keep utilization below 30% of your limit. They hurt your score when you carry balances at 20%+ APR or miss payments. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on day one of the card to protect your payment history, then aim to pay the full statement balance each cycle.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best travel credit card overall?
The Chase Sapphire Preferred is the best overall travel credit card for most travelers. It earns 3x on travel and dining, charges no foreign transaction fees, includes trip cancellation and delay insurance, and offers a 60K point welcome bonus worth $750+ in travel. The $95 annual fee is easy to justify with just moderate travel spending.
Do travel credit cards charge foreign transaction fees?
The best travel credit cards charge no foreign transaction fees. All five cards in our rankings -- Chase Sapphire Preferred, Capital One Venture X, Wells Fargo Autograph, Amex Gold, and Capital One Venture -- charge 0% on purchases made outside the United States. Cards that do charge foreign transaction fees typically add 3% to every overseas purchase, which adds up quickly on international trips.
What travel insurance do credit cards provide?
Premium travel cards typically provide trip cancellation insurance ($5,000-$10,000 per trip), trip delay reimbursement ($300-$500 for delays over 6-12 hours), lost luggage coverage ($1,500-$3,000), and rental car collision damage waivers. The Chase Sapphire Preferred and Capital One Venture X offer the most comprehensive travel insurance packages among cards under $400/year.
What is the best no-fee travel credit card?
The Wells Fargo Autograph is the best no-fee travel card with 3x on travel, dining, gas, streaming, and phone plans. It charges no annual fee and no foreign transaction fees, making it ideal for budget-conscious travelers who still want strong rewards. The Capital One VentureOne (1.25x on everything, no fee) is another solid option for travelers who prefer simplicity.
Are travel credit card points worth more than cash back?
Travel points are often worth more than cash back when redeemed through airline and hotel transfer partners. Chase Ultimate Rewards points are worth 1.25-2 cents each when transferred to partners like Hyatt or United, compared to 1 cent as cash back. However, cash back is simpler and guaranteed in value. If you travel at least twice per year, points typically deliver better value.
What is the best card for international travel?
The Chase Sapphire Preferred is the best card for international travel because it combines no foreign transaction fees, 3x on all travel and dining worldwide, comprehensive trip insurance, and points that transfer to 14 international airline partners. The Capital One Venture X is also excellent for international travel with Priority Pass lounge access and no foreign fees.
How do travel credit card points transfer to airlines?
Cards with transferable points allow you to move points to airline frequent flyer programs at a 1:1 ratio (or better in some cases). For example, you can transfer 60,000 Chase points to 60,000 United MileagePlus miles and book a round-trip international flight worth $800-$1,200. Transfers are typically instant and give you more value than redeeming points as cash back.
Should I get a general travel card or an airline-specific card?
General travel cards with transferable points (like Chase Sapphire or Capital One Venture) are better for most travelers because they offer flexibility -- you are not locked into one airline. Airline-specific cards make sense only if you fly the same airline 80%+ of the time and want perks like free checked bags and priority boarding on that specific carrier.
