What to Know

How Boston Businesses Access Capital

Small businesses in Boston, Massachusetts have multiple funding pathways. Traditional bank loans work for established businesses with 2+ years of financials and 680+ credit. SBA loans offer lower rates and longer terms but take 30-90 days. Online and alternative lenders approve in 24-72 hours with more flexible credit requirements, trading speed for slightly higher APR. The right fit depends on your time-in-business, revenue, and how fast you need the money.

Documents to Have Ready Before You Apply

Most lenders want: 3-6 months of business bank statements, the two most recent business tax returns, a current profit-and-loss statement, proof of business registration or articles of incorporation, a voided business check, and a photo ID for the signer. Having these uploaded before the application starts cuts the time-to-funding by several business days.

What to Look For Beyond the Headline Rate

Advertised APRs are only part of the cost. Always check: origination fees (typically 1-5% of the loan amount, built into APR on a true-APR quote), prepayment penalties (can trap you if you want to pay off early), daily or weekly repayment schedules (which compress cash flow), and personal guarantee requirements (standard for most small-business loans under $250K). A higher APR with no prepayment penalty is often cheaper than a lower APR with a 2% prepayment fee.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do I need for a business loan in Boston, MA?
Credit score requirements depend on lender and product. SBA loans through the Massachusetts District Office generally want 680+ personal credit. Boston-area banks (Eastern Bank, Rockland Trust, Cambridge Savings) typically look for 650+ on conventional term loans. CDFIs like the Massachusetts Growth Capital Corporation (MGCC) and the Boston Local Development Corporation serve borrowers who may not qualify at banks, focusing on revenue, cash flow, and the lender's mission alignment.
How does Massachusetts Corporate Excise Tax affect Boston business loans?
Massachusetts levies a Corporate Excise Tax combining a net income portion (8% of net income attributable to MA) and a non-income portion (0.26% of tangible property or net worth, whichever is higher, minimum $456). Lenders factor this state-level burden into operating expense projections. Boston businesses operating as S-corporations or LLCs taxed as partnerships face different state treatment than C-corporations — specialist MA lenders understand the distinctions; generalist lenders sometimes misclassify the burden on pro formas.
How fast can I get a business loan approved in Boston?
Approval speed varies by loan type. SBA 7(a) loans through the Massachusetts District Office typically run 30-90 days. Boston bank term loans from the regional institutions average 2-4 weeks. Online and alternative lenders deliver same-day decisions with funds in 1-3 business days — useful for Boston's hospitality and retail businesses facing seasonal cash flow cycles or the city's compressed commercial real estate market.
What types of business loans are available in Boston?
Boston businesses access SBA 7(a) and 504 loans through the Massachusetts District Office, conventional term loans from Boston-area banks (Eastern Bank, Rockland Trust, Cambridge Savings, Brookline Bank), business lines of credit, equipment financing (heavy in Boston's biotech and medical device corridors), commercial real estate loans (particularly relevant given Boston's expensive CRE market), invoice factoring, and merchant cash advances. CDFIs include Massachusetts Growth Capital Corporation (MGCC), Boston Local Development Corporation, and CommonWealth Kitchen for food businesses.
Are there Boston-specific resources for small business financing?
Yes. The Massachusetts Growth Capital Corporation (MGCC) is a major public-quasi-public CDFI funding small businesses across MA with loans from $15K to $1M. The Boston Local Development Corporation provides microloans and small business capital specifically for Boston-based businesses. The Massachusetts Small Business Development Center Network (MSBDC) offers free advising through hosts like Salem State and UMass. The Boston Main Streets program supports neighborhood business districts. CommonWealth Kitchen serves Boston's growing food entrepreneur base with lending and incubation.
