What to Know

How Seattle Businesses Access Capital

Small businesses in Seattle, Washington have multiple funding pathways. Traditional bank loans work for established businesses with 2+ years of financials and 680+ credit. SBA loans offer lower rates and longer terms but take 30-90 days. Online and alternative lenders approve in 24-72 hours with more flexible credit requirements, trading speed for slightly higher APR. The right fit depends on your time-in-business, revenue, and how fast you need the money.

Documents to Have Ready Before You Apply

Most lenders want: 3-6 months of business bank statements, the two most recent business tax returns, a current profit-and-loss statement, proof of business registration or articles of incorporation, a voided business check, and a photo ID for the signer. Having these uploaded before the application starts cuts the time-to-funding by several business days.

What to Look For Beyond the Headline Rate

Advertised APRs are only part of the cost. Always check: origination fees (typically 1-5% of the loan amount, built into APR on a true-APR quote), prepayment penalties (can trap you if you want to pay off early), daily or weekly repayment schedules (which compress cash flow), and personal guarantee requirements (standard for most small-business loans under $250K). A higher APR with no prepayment penalty is often cheaper than a lower APR with a 2% prepayment fee.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Washington's B&O tax affect my business loan application?
Washington's Business & Occupation (B&O) tax is a gross receipts tax rather than an income tax. Lenders understand this structure and will not penalize you for it. In fact, because Washington has no state income tax, many Seattle businesses retain more net revenue, which can strengthen your loan application. Be prepared to show your B&O tax filings and City of Seattle B&O tax records as part of the documentation process.
Does Washington's lack of state income tax help me qualify for a business loan?
Yes, Washington's zero state income tax can be a meaningful advantage. With no state income tax reducing your take-home revenue, your business may show stronger cash flow compared to similar businesses in states with 5%–10% income tax rates. Lenders evaluate net cash flow heavily, so Seattle businesses often present more favorable financial profiles. You will still need to account for the state B&O tax and Seattle's city B&O tax in your financials.
What funding options exist for Seattle tech startups?
Seattle's tech ecosystem offers multiple funding paths. Beyond traditional venture capital and angel investors, tech startups can access SBA microloans through local intermediaries, revenue-based financing from alternative lenders once they have 3+ months of revenue, and programs through Washington SBDC and Business Impact NW. Craft3, a Pacific Northwest CDFI, also provides loans specifically for innovative and mission-driven businesses. Many alternative lenders serving the Seattle market fund startups with as little as $10,000 in monthly revenue.
What local lending programs are available to Seattle small businesses?
Seattle businesses have access to several local and regional programs. The Washington Small Business Development Center (SBDC) provides free consulting and connects businesses with lenders. Craft3 is a Pacific Northwest CDFI offering loans from $25,000 to $3 million. Business Impact NW provides microloans and small business loans with flexible terms. Community Capital Development offers SBA 504 loans for real estate and equipment. The SBA Seattle District Office also maintains a network of preferred lenders throughout the Puget Sound region.
What types of business loans are available in the Seattle metro area?
Seattle-area businesses can access SBA 7(a) and 504 loans, traditional bank term loans, business lines of credit, equipment financing, merchant cash advances, invoice factoring, revenue-based financing, and commercial real estate loans. The region's strong tech and maritime industries also support specialized financing products like venture debt, startup-friendly revenue-based advances, and equipment loans for maritime and construction operations.
